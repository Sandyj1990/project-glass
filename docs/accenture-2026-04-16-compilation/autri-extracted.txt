1. The use case: Planogram Compliance
Planogram compliance is the use case. Everything in this document, every system, every
handoff, every metric, exists to serve that one goal.
A planogram is the visual plan of what goes where on the shelf: which SKU sits on which
fixture, at which shelf level, how many facings, and at what price. Compliance means the
live shelf matches that plan in every store, all day, every day.
In grocery, non compliance shows up as out of stocks on high velocity SKUs, wrong
products on wrong shelves, incorrect facings, expired or wrong price tags, and missed
promotional displays. Each of these has a direct hit on revenue, customer trust, and vendor
contracts. Today, this is managed through manual walks, spot audits, and reactive fixes.
That is what we are replacing.

2. What we want to achieve
Run planogram compliance as a continuous, autonomous loop across the grocery estate.
The store gets scanned, the system identifies what is off, the right person gets a specific
task to fix it, and the next scan confirms whether the fix held.
The outcome is a self verifying loop that catches every compliance gap, closes it with the
right person, and proves it was actually fixed. Fewer out of stocks on high velocity SKUs,
promotional price errors caught inside the hour, and Cluster Managers spending their time
on the stores that actually need them.
3. Autri: Today vs. The Agentic Flow
Autri is already deployed and scanning shelves. Today, it operates as a standalone data
collection and analytics tool. In this plan, it becomes the autonomous eyes of a closed-loop
agentic workflow.
3.1 How Autri operates today
Today, Autri runs scheduled scan sessions across the store, capturing high-resolution
images of every fixture. It uses its own AI models to draw bounding boxes around
recognized SKUs, matching them against its internal SKU master.
Autri's current dashboard showing session scheduling and completion status.

The output today is a set of analytics dashboards showing brand occupancy and facing
heatmaps. While this provides visibility, it requires a human to log in, interpret the
heatmap, figure out what is wrong, and manually tell someone to fix it.
Current analytics output: Brand by shelf level and facing count heatmaps.
3.2 How Autri will operate in the Agentic Flow
In the new architecture, Autri stops being an analytics dashboard and becomes a pure, high-
fidelity data producer for the Agentic Command Center.
  1. No more human interpretation: Autri's tagged shelf captures flow directly into Gmetri
     via API.
  2. Context-aware scanning: Instead of generic daily scans, Autri's schedule is
     dynamically tied to the store's restocking windows and promotional calendar.
  3. The closer of the loop: Autri's most important new role is verification. When a staff
     member marks a PulsePoint ticket as "Done", the ticket stays open until Autri's next
     scan of that specific fixture confirms the compliance gap is actually resolved.

8. Design choices specific to grocery
Three choices make this compliance loop work for the grocery format.
Velocity weighted severity. An empty slot for a top velocity SKU is not the same as a facing
drift on a slow mover. The Command Center weights triage by SKU velocity, not by raw
count of violations. This keeps the store manager's PulsePoint inbox focused on what
actually hits revenue and customer experience.
Promotional execution is its own lane. Promotions drive a disproportionate share of
grocery sales and customer trust. Price mismatches and missing end caps during an active
promotion window are flagged Critical and go as push, regardless of the underlying
compliance dimension. This lane has its own SLA.
Scans timed to restocking. Compliance signal is only meaningful after restocking has
happened. Autri scans are scheduled immediately after the primary restocking windows so
that the alerts reflect post restock reality. This single choice removes a large class of false
alerts and earns trust with store staff from day one.
